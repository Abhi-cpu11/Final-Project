Transitioning from Player to Coach

In Grade 8 at HGI, I was one of the lower-level players. I was getting minutes but not making much of an impact. Over the summer, I put in serious work and came back a completely different player. By Grade 9, not only was I performing at a high level, but I also started stepping into a leadership role. I began drawing up plays that worked almost every time. I could read the game and sense when it was the right moment to run a certain action or shift the defense. That’s when I started thinking like a coach, even though I was still a player.

Student of the Game

Even when I was playing, I spent more time studying the game than most people around me. After practice, I would go home and watch NBA or college games, not just for fun, but to actually learn something. I would pause the video, rewind plays, and break down the small things. I looked at how defenders rotated when they were off the ball, how timing affected spacing, or how a jab step could shift the entire defense. Watching film became a habit, and it changed how I saw the game. That mindset shaped the way I played. I was never out there just running around or sticking to a script. I tried to make sense of what was happening in real time and react with purpose. Now as a coach, I want my players to see the game the same way. I don’t just teach plays or moves. I teach the meaning behind them. I want my team or players to know when to adjust, when to read the defense, and why every detail matters.
